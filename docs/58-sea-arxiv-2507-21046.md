# 58 — SEA Survey: "What, When, How, Where to Evolve" — a Map of Self-Evolving Agents

**Paper.** "A Survey of Self-Evolving Agents: What, When, How, and Where to Evolve on the Path to Artificial Super Intelligence" — Gao et al., [arXiv:2507.21046](https://arxiv.org/abs/2507.21046).
**Authors / affiliation.** 27 authors led by Huan-ang Gao, including Qingyun Wu, Heng Ji, Mengdi Wang. Affiliations are not consolidated on the arXiv abstract page; the companion repo is maintained by CharlesQ9. The paper is *published in Transactions on Machine Learning Research* (01/2026), reported by the authors on the arXiv page.
**Dates.** Submitted 28 Jul 2025 (v1); last revised 16 Jan 2026 (v4); 77 pages, 9 figures.
**Companion repo.** [github.com/CharlesQ9/Self-Evolving-Agents](https://github.com/CharlesQ9/Self-Evolving-Agents) (~1.1k stars at time of review).

## What the paper is about (one paragraph)

This is the first large-scale *organizing* survey of self-evolving agents (SEAs) — systems where the agent itself modifies some part of its own machinery (weights, context, tools, architecture) based on its own trajectories or feedback, so that it improves on future tasks. The paper's entire contribution is a **four-axis taxonomy** — *what, when, how, where* to evolve — under which it collects and classifies ~100+ systems (Voyager, Reflexion, ADAS, AFlow, Darwin Gödel Machine, Memento, Mem0, ALITA, AlphaEvolve, SkillWeaver, PromptBreeder, EvoAgent, TextGrad, DSPy, and many more). It then assembles a matching evaluation vocabulary (adaptivity, retention, generalization, efficiency, safety) and surveys open challenges in safety, scalability, personalization, and co-evolutionary multi-agent dynamics. It is a map, not a method; its value to the harness engineer is the shared vocabulary and the cross-system comparisons it enables.

## Contribution claims (as the paper and companion site state)

Distilled from the abstract, GitHub README, and paper HTML. Quoted / paraphrased as noted.

- "**First systematic and comprehensive review** of self-evolving agents, organized around three foundational dimensions — *what* to evolve, *when* to evolve, and *how* to evolve" (abstract, arXiv).
- Examination of "evolutionary mechanisms across agent components (e.g., **models, memory, tools, architecture**)" — the four pillars of *what* (abstract).
- "Categorization of adaptation methods by **stages across the agent's lifecycle**" — i.e., intra-test-time vs inter-test-time evolution (*when*; abstract).
- "Analysis of algorithmic and architectural designs that guide the evolution process" — reward-based / imitation / population-based (*how*; abstract).
- A dedicated "**where**" axis added in v4 (title page, v3→v4): general-purpose domain vs specialized domains (coding, GUI, financial, medical, education, games).
- A proposed evaluation framework around five axes: **adaptivity, retention, generalization, efficiency, safety** (paper §Evaluation).
- Enumeration of open challenges: personalization, generalization, safety & control, multi-agent ecosystems (§Challenges).

No new benchmark, model, or method is proposed. This is a classification paper.

## Method — the four-axis map

### 1. What to evolve (four pillars)

| Pillar | Subcategory | Representative systems the paper places here |
|---|---|---|
| **Models** | Policy evolution (weight updates from self-supervision) | Self-Challenging Agent (SCA), Self-Rewarding Self-Improving |
| **Models** | Experience evolution (learn from constructed env) | AgentGen, Reflexion, AdaPlanner, SICA, RAGEN, DYSTIL |
| **Context — Memory** | Store / retrieve / forget | SAGE, A-Mem, **Mem0**, Memory-R1, MemInsight, REMEMBER, **Memento**, MemGen, **ReasoningBank**, Agent Workflow Memory, MUSE |
| **Context — Prompt** | Instruction / prompt optimization | APE, ORPO, ADO, ProTeGi, **PromptAgent**, **PromptBreeder**, REVOLVE, SPO, **ACE**, **DSPy**, **TextGrad**, LLM-AutoDiff, MASS, MAS-ZERO, EvoAgent, AgentSquare |
| **Tools** | Autonomous creation | **Voyager**, ALITA, CREATOR, CRAFT, APIBench |
| **Tools** | Mastery by refinement | **SkillWeaver**, LearnAct, DRAFT |
| **Tools** | Management & selection | ToolGen, ToolBench, API-Bank |
| **Architecture** | Single-agent workflow opt | TRACE, prompt-refinement methods |
| **Architecture** | Multi-agent workflow opt | ReMA, MASS, MAS-ZERO, Multi-Agent Design |

Reading this table against our corpus is the main pay-off: Voyager ([19](19-voyager-skill-libraries.md)) is a *Tools → autonomous creation* system; Hermes Agent ([55](55-hermes-agent-self-improving.md)) sits in *Tools → autonomous creation* **plus** *Memory → store/retrieve*; Autogenesis ([36](36-autogenesis-self-evolving-agents.md)) is the only system spanning all four pillars by protocolizing them; Hyperagents / DGM-H ([45](45-hyperagents-self-modification.md)) live in *Architecture → single-agent* and — uniquely — edit the *mechanism* that edits the other pillars.

### 2. When to evolve (two temporal regimes)

- **Intra-test-time** — updates occur *within* a single task: in-context learning (ICL), SFT-at-test-time, online RL over the current rollout.
- **Inter-test-time** — updates occur *between* tasks: persistent ICL demonstration accumulation, batched SFT, multi-task RL.

This axis is the paper's most practically useful distinction for a harness engineer. It cleanly separates Reflexion-style per-task reflection (intra) from Voyager-style skill-library accumulation (inter), and exposes hybrids like Hermes's "self-eval every 15 tasks" cadence as explicit *inter*-time updates.

### 3. How to evolve (three methodological families)

- **Reward-based** — textual feedback (SELF, SCoRe, PAG, TextGrad, AutoRule), internal rewards, external rewards, implicit rewards.
- **Imitation / demonstration** — self-generated, cross-agent, hybrid. Reflexion is the canonical self-generated case.
- **Population-based / evolutionary** — single-agent variants (PromptBreeder, evolutionary prompt search) and multi-agent (**EvoAgent**, **AgentSquare**).

Cross-cutting axes within *how*: online vs offline, on-policy vs off-policy, and reward granularity (task / step / trajectory).

### 4. Where to evolve (v4 addition)

- **General-domain** evolution — memory mechanism enhancement, model-agent co-evolution, curriculum-driven training.
- **Specialized domain** — Coding (**AlphaEvolve**, SICA), GUI (SkillWeaver), Financial, Medical, Education, plus Games (Voyager → Minecraft; Richelieu → Diplomacy).

### Operational definition (from §2)

Stated by the paper: a self-evolving agent "modifies its internal parameters, contextual state, toolset, or architectural topology based on its own trajectories or feedback signals, with the explicit objective of improving future performance," subject to three inclusion criteria: updates are **experience-dependent**, produce **persistent policy-changing effects**, and originate from **autonomous exploration mechanisms**.

## Experimental setup and reported numbers

Because this is a survey, there are **no newly reported benchmark numbers** that belong to the paper itself. Everything quantitative in it is borrowed from the surveyed systems. The paper's own empirical contribution is the classification and the suggested *evaluation framework*, not measurements.

The evaluation framework the paper advocates, reported by authors:

- **Adaptivity** — improvement on new tasks after evolution.
- **Retention** — knowledge preservation across evolutionary updates (i.e., no catastrophic forgetting).
- **Generalization** — transfer across domains / distributions.
- **Efficiency** — compute / token cost of the evolution itself, not just inference.
- **Safety** — robustness and alignment preserved through updates.

Evaluation *paradigms* recommended (paper §Evaluation, reported by authors):

- Static assessment (external task solving; internal component eval).
- Short-horizon adaptive assessment (augmented traditional benchmarks; dynamic evaluation).
- Long-horizon lifelong-learning assessment.
- Standardized evaluation protocols.

Any specific number — "Voyager unlocks 3× more tech-tree items than prior agents," "DGM improves coding score by X" — appears in this survey *only as a citation of the original work*, not as a new measurement. Practitioners should chase the primary references for numeric evidence.

## Harness-engineering implications

The taxonomy becomes useful the moment you try to place our corpus on it. Here is how a harness engineer should read the map:

- **Hermes Agent ([55](55-hermes-agent-self-improving.md))** is a *Tools ∪ Memory* inter-test-time system using imitation-from-own-trajectories — its "store what worked" skill-library pattern lands cleanly on *What: Tools → autonomous creation* + *How: self-generated demonstration* + *When: inter-test-time*. The survey's explicit split between *memory evolution* (Mem0, A-mem, Memento, ReasoningBank) and *tool evolution* (Voyager, ALITA) clarifies that Hermes actually spans both, and that its "procedural memory as Markdown skills" is the unification.
- **Voyager ([19](19-voyager-skill-libraries.md))** is the paper's canonical *Tools → autonomous creation* + *How: population-free self-evolution* example; almost every subsequent skill-library paper is a variant.
- **SemaClaw ([54](54-semaclaw-general-purpose-agent.md))** is *not* a self-evolving agent under the paper's operational definition — its DAG teams, PermissionBridge, and SOUL.md are static harness affordances. SemaClaw's "agentic wiki skill" is the only piece that comes close to inter-test-time context evolution. This matters: teams building "self-improving personal agents" often conflate *remember* (SemaClaw) with *evolve* (Hermes / Voyager / Autogenesis); the survey's inclusion criteria (experience-dependent, persistent policy-changing, autonomous exploration) are a precise tripwire for that conflation.
- **Autogenesis ([36](36-autogenesis-self-evolving-agents.md))** is an *Architecture*-pillar contribution but with a *meta* twist: it supplies the versioning/rollback protocol that *every* pillar can safely use. The SEA survey has no explicit slot for "evolution infrastructure as protocol," which is an interesting gap — the closest categorical match is *Architecture → single-agent optimization*, but Autogenesis is really a substrate for the whole map.
- **Hyperagents / DGM-H ([45](45-hyperagents-self-modification.md))** is the paper's cleanest example of *evolving the evolution mechanism itself* — a case the survey acknowledges (via DGM, Gödel Agent) but doesn't treat as its own axis. For harness engineers this is a strong hint that a fifth axis — **depth of self-modification (behavior / mechanism / meta-mechanism)** — is under-developed in the map.
- **Adaptation of Agentic AI survey ([47](47-adaptation-of-agentic-ai-survey.md))** organizes adaptation by *who supervises what* (T1: model-supervised agent; T2: agent-supervised tool; etc.). The SEA survey asks *what changes*; [47] asks *who drives the change*. Using both together is more informative than either alone — e.g., "Hermes = SEA (Tools+Memory, inter-test-time, imitation) × Adaptation (T2)."
- **Skills ([04](04-skills.md)), Memory files ([09](09-memory-files.md)), Reflexion ([14](14-reflexion.md)), Context compaction ([08](08-context-compaction.md))** — each is a *primitive* that a given SEA pillar depends on. The survey talks about memory eviction / forgetting in the abstract; our [08](08-context-compaction.md) is the concrete harness implementation that memory-evolution systems sit on top of.
- The paper's *intra* vs *inter* test-time split matches our [11](11-verifier-evaluator-loops.md) (intra) and [19](19-voyager-skill-libraries.md) (inter) distinction, and gives us a cleaner name for it.

The one-line takeaway: **the survey is a shared vocabulary, not a blueprint.** Use it to classify what you're already building; use it to notice which pillar you have *not* been evolving (tools? memory? architecture?) and whether that's a deliberate choice.

## Limitations and open questions

Admitted by the authors (§Challenges, reported verbatim-adjacent):

- **Personalization** — data governance, user-specific evaluation.
- **Generalization** — catastrophic forgetting, cross-domain transfer, scalable architecture design.
- **Safety & control** — emergent risks from self-modification, prescriptive guardrails (this intersects our [22](22-guardrails-prompt-injection.md), [26](26-linuxarena-production-agent-safety.md), [49](49-agents-of-chaos-red-teaming.md)).
- **Multi-agent ecosystems** — individual vs collective reasoning, efficient frameworks, dynamic evaluation.

Observed by this reviewer, not stated in the paper:

- **No empirical axis.** Because there is no new experiment, the classification stands or falls on its internal coherence. Several systems appear in multiple cells (e.g., EvoAgent under both *Prompt optimization* and *Population-based*); the taxonomy is overlapping, not partitioning.
- **Missing "depth" axis.** Behavior-level, mechanism-level, and meta-mechanism-level self-modification are all collapsed into *Architecture*; [45](45-hyperagents-self-modification.md)'s metacognitive editing deserves its own dimension.
- **Evolution infrastructure under-mapped.** Autogenesis-style versioned resources, rollback gates, and lineage are operational necessities for any inter-test-time SEA in production. The survey discusses them as "safety" only.
- **No treatment of cost-of-evolution in $.** Efficiency is one of the five evaluation axes, but operationalized as compute, not as token-bill per skill learned. Hermes-style cache-aware skill resolution or Rebalance ([51](51-rebalance-efficient-reasoning.md))-style efficient reasoning would be natural measurement targets.
- **"Where to evolve" is thin** in v4 — coding and GUI dominate; financial and medical get a paragraph each. A follow-up survey specializing on vertical self-evolution (e.g., RadAgent [28](28-radagent-agentic-radiology.md)) is an obvious opening.
- **Definition inclusion boundary.** The three criteria (experience-dependent / persistent / autonomous) exclude OpenClaw ([52](52-dive-into-open-claw.md))-style memory-only systems. Whether that line should be drawn there is arguable; some practitioners would call a memory-only system self-evolving.

## What a practitioner should copy vs leave behind

**Copy:**

- **The four pillars as a planning checklist.** When you design a harness that claims to "learn over time," explicitly state which of {models, memory, tools, architecture} evolves, and which do not. If "none," you are not building an SEA — call it something else.
- **Intra vs inter test-time** as an explicit config flag on every learning mechanism. Confusing the two is the single most common mistake in self-improving-agent demos.
- **The five evaluation axes** (adaptivity, retention, generalization, efficiency, safety) as your dashboard. Hold yourself to reporting all five; showing adaptivity without retention is the failure mode this whole survey exists to name.
- **The operational definition's three criteria.** Use them as a tripwire against "self-improving" marketing claims (especially: is the update *persistent*? is it *autonomous*?).
- **Cross-linking discipline.** The paper's strongest move is to place every system on a shared grid. Do the same inside your organization — a single table of "what your agents evolve" is astonishingly useful.

**Leave behind:**

- **Treating the taxonomy as exhaustive.** It is a snapshot of pre-2026 literature; depth-of-self-modification, evolution infrastructure, and cost-per-skill are not cleanly represented. Extend the grid with your own columns.
- **Taking surveyed numbers as verified.** Every numeric claim was run by the *original* authors, not re-validated here. Pull from primary sources before citing.
- **Assuming *Architecture* evolution is always desirable.** Most production harnesses (especially safety-critical ones — see [26](26-linuxarena-production-agent-safety.md)) should hold architecture fixed and evolve only memory / tools. The paper is theory-neutral; practice is not.
- **The AGI framing.** The "Path to Artificial Super Intelligence" subtitle is aspirational and does no work in the classification. Ignore it when using the map operationally.
