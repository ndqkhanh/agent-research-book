# 59 — SEA: A Comprehensive Survey of Self-Evolving AI Agents

**TLDR.** *A Comprehensive Survey of Self-Evolving AI Agents: A New Paradigm Bridging Foundation Models and Lifelong Agentic Systems* ([arXiv:2508.07407](https://arxiv.org/abs/2508.07407), v1 submitted 2025-08-10, v2 revised 2025-08-31) proposes a **unified four-component feedback-loop framework** — System Inputs (ℐ), Agent System (𝒜), Environment (ℰ), Optimisers (𝒪) — and a four-paradigm maturity curve (**MOP → MOA → MAO → MASE**) for reasoning about how every part of an agent — foundation model weights, prompts, memory, tools, workflow topology, inter-agent communication — can be made to evolve after deployment. The survey frames evolution as a search problem, 𝒜\* = arg max<sub>𝒜∈𝒮</sub> 𝒪(𝒜; ℐ), catalogues single-agent and multi-agent optimisation techniques, reviews domain-specific strategies in biomedicine, programming, and finance/legal, and proposes the **"Three Laws of Self-Evolving Agents"** (Endure / Excel / Evolve). This is a taxonomy paper, not a benchmark paper; its utility for harness engineers is as a vocabulary and coverage map.

**Authors.** Jinyuan Fang, Yanwen Peng, Xi Zhang, Yingxu Wang, Xinhao Yi, Guibin Zhang, Yi Xu, Bin Wu, Siwei Liu, Zihao Li, Zhaochun Ren, Nikos Aletras, Xi Wang, Han Zhou, Zaiqiao Meng. Primary affiliation pointer: Zaiqiao Meng (University of Glasgow / EvoAgentX project lead); affiliations are not exhaustively enumerated on the arxiv abstract page. Companion GitHub: [EvoAgentX/Awesome-Self-Evolving-Agents](https://github.com/EvoAgentX/Awesome-Self-Evolving-Agents) and the executable framework [EvoAgentX/EvoAgentX](https://github.com/EvoAgentX/EvoAgentX). License CC-BY-4.0.

**Date posture for this file.** Written 2026-04-20; the survey is ~8 months old and its ~50-paper corpus predates most of the self-improving harnesses we catalogue in files [36](36-autogenesis-self-evolving-agents.md), [45](45-hyperagents-self-modification.md), [54](54-semaclaw-general-purpose-agent.md), and [55](55-hermes-agent-self-improving.md). Read it as *the map* the 2026 practitioner work has been filling in.

## Contribution claims (as stated)

- A **unified conceptual framework** abstracting the feedback loop of self-evolving agentic systems into four components — *System Inputs*, *Agent System*, *Environment*, *Optimisers* — "serving as a foundation for understanding and comparing different strategies."
- A **systematic review** of self-evolving techniques that target different components of the agent system: foundation models, agent prompts, memory, tools, workflows, and inter-agent communication mechanisms.
- A **paradigm-evolution axis** — MOP (Model Offline Pretraining) → MOA (Model Online Adaptation) → MAO (Multi-Agent Orchestration) → MASE (Multi-Agent Self-Evolving) — which positions current work and frames what "lifelong" agentic systems require.
- The **Three Laws of Self-Evolving AI Agents** (a nod to Asimov): **I. Endure** — preserve safety and stability during self-modification; **II. Excel** — preserve or improve existing task performance; **III. Evolve** — autonomously optimise components in response to changing tasks/environments. Any self-evolution technique is to be judged against these three in priority order.
- **Domain-specific evolution strategies** reviewed for biomedicine, programming, and finance/legal, where "agent behaviour and optimisation objectives are tightly coupled with domain constraints."
- A **dedicated treatment** of evaluation, safety, and ethical considerations for self-evolving systems — "critical to ensuring their effectiveness and reliability."
- A **living companion repository** (Awesome-Self-Evolving-Agents) that organises "over 50 cutting-edge papers from premier venues" into the taxonomy.

## Method — the framework

### The optimisation formalism

The survey formalises every self-evolving agent technique as:

```
𝒜* = argmax_{𝒜 ∈ 𝒮} 𝒪(𝒜; ℐ)
```

where **𝒮** is the search space of agent configurations, **𝒪(𝒜; ℐ)** is an evaluation function mapping configuration-on-inputs to a scalar, and **𝒜\*** is the optimised configuration. An optimiser is then the pair (search space 𝒮, algorithm ℋ), with ℋ drawn from "rule-based heuristics, gradient descent, Bayesian optimization, MCTS, RL, evolutionary algorithms." This is the unifying trick: reflection, prompt-gradient, AFlow-style code search, and DGM-style meta-evolution are all *one formalism* with different 𝒮 and ℋ.

### The four components

| Component | Symbol | Definition (survey) |
|-----------|--------|---------------------|
| System Inputs | ℐ | Task descriptions and datasets |
| Agent System | 𝒜 | LLM, prompts, memory, tools (single or multi-agent) |
| Environment | ℰ | Operational context generating feedback signals |
| Optimisers | 𝒪 | Search algorithms refining agent configurations |

Note: the survey reuses the 𝒪 symbol for both the objective and the optimiser class; the text disambiguates from context.

### The MOP → MOA → MAO → MASE axis

- **MOP** — static frozen models deployed as-is.
- **MOA** — post-deployment fine-tuning of the base model (SFT, LoRA, RLHF).
- **MAO** — coordinated multi-agent systems with **fixed** protocols.
- **MASE** — **continuous autonomous refinement** of prompts, memory, tools, topologies (what this survey is really about).

MASE is the terminus and the open research frontier. Most surveyed work lives in MOA or MAO; a smaller cohort reaches MASE.

### Taxonomy of techniques (companion-repo structure)

**Single-agent optimisation**
- *LLM behaviour* — training-based (STaR, ToRA, NExT, DeepSeek-Prover-v1.5, Self-Rewarding); test-time scaling (CoT-SC, Tree-of-Thoughts, Graph-of-Thoughts, Forest-of-Thought).
- *Prompt optimisation* — edit-based (GPS, GrIPS, TEMPERA); generative (APE, PromptAgent, OPRO, MIPRO, StraGo, SPO); text-gradient (ProTeGi, TextGrad); evolutionary (EvoPrompt, Promptbreeder).
- *Memory optimisation* — short-term (COMEDY, ReadAgent, MoT, StructRAG, MemoryBank); long-term (EWE, A-MEM, Mem0, HippoRAG, GraphReader, AWM, MemGPT).
- *Tool optimisation* — training-based (ToolLLM, Confucius, ReTool, ToolRL, Nemotron-Research-Tool-N1); prompt-based (EASYTOOL, DRAFT, PLAY2PROMPT); tool creation (CREATOR, LATM, CRAFT, AgentOptimizer, Alita).

**Multi-agent optimisation**
- *Topology & workflow* — code-level (AutoFlow, AFlow, ScoreFlow, MAS-GPT); communication-graph (GPTSwarm, DynaSwarm, G-Designer, AgentPrune); unified (ADAS, EvoAgent, MASS, EvoFlow, MAS-ZERO).
- *Agent-centric* — MorphAgent, EvoAgent, Agent Square, Hetero Swarms, AutoAgents, DSPy.

**Domain-specific optimisation**
- *Biomedicine* — MDAgents, MedAgentSim, MDTeamGPT, MMedAgent; molecular — CACTUS, ChemAgent, DrugAgent.
- *Programming* — Self-Refine, AgentCoder, CodeAgent; debugging — Self-Debugging, PyCapsule, UniDebug.
- *Finance/legal* — FinRobot, PEER, FinCon; LawLuo, AgentCourt, LegalGPT.
- Plus *scientific research*, *climate*, *computer-use* coverage.

### The self-improvement loop (the primitive)

Across all the methods above, the survey synthesises one loop primitive: **feedback from ℰ → optimiser 𝒪 proposes change in 𝒮 → evaluate on ℐ → commit or discard**. The novelty across methods is *what 𝒮 contains* (only prompts? tools? whole topologies? the optimiser itself?) and *what ℋ uses to search* it.

## Experimental setup and reported numbers

**None — and be honest about it.** This is a *survey*, not a method paper. It reports no new benchmark runs of its own. Numbers in the referenced works (e.g., AFlow's cost/accuracy ratios, Voyager's ~3× tech-tree coverage noted in [19](19-voyager-skill-libraries.md), TextGrad's prompt-search gains) are cited inside cells of the taxonomy but not re-verified. **Section 4 (Evaluation)** does enumerate the evaluation landscape:

- **Benchmark-based evaluation** across tool-driven agents, web navigation, coding, research, and multimodal environments.
- **LLM-based evaluation** — judge models, agent-as-judge ([21](21-llm-as-judge-trajectory-eval.md)).
- **Safety & alignment** — robustness testing aimed specifically at *lifelong* agents, where the system under test changes between runs.

If you want a number to quote from this paper, the cleanest one is the **~50+ surveyed works** organised into the taxonomy (companion-repo-level claim; the paper itself does not print a precise N). Beyond that, resist the temptation to "borrow" numbers — go to the primary sources.

## Harness-engineering implications

### Compared to Hermes Agent ([55](55-hermes-agent-self-improving.md))

Hermes is the cleanest **single-agent MASE instance** of 2026: write-what-worked skill extraction, three-layer memory (procedural / preference / session + optional Honcho), a 15-task self-eval cadence. Under the SEA framework: Hermes's 𝒮 is *procedural-memory markdown + preference store*, its ℋ is *LLM-driven pattern extraction with post-hoc self-eval*, its ℰ is *real user traffic over Telegram/Slack/email*. SEA's Law I (Endure) is implicit in Hermes (skills are additive, not replacing core tooling); Law II (Excel) is enforced by the periodic self-eval; Law III (Evolve) is the whole point. SEA's value here is **showing Hermes is one point in a much bigger design space** — memory-only evolution is the conservative slice.

### Compared to Voyager ([19](19-voyager-skill-libraries.md))

Voyager is the *original* working MASE-lite system in the taxonomy's sense — automatic curriculum (proposer of ℐ), code-prompting loop (ℋ over 𝒮 = skill library), environment feedback (ℰ = Minecraft). SEA retrofits this into its four-component view cleanly: Voyager doesn't evolve prompts, tools (beyond code skills), or topology; it evolves **one dimension** — the skill library. Almost everything else in the survey's taxonomy is "Voyager but for a different 𝒮."

### Compared to SemaClaw ([54](54-semaclaw-general-purpose-agent.md))

SemaClaw is deliberately **not MASE**: it emphasises *deterministic scheduling*, *PermissionBridge* as runtime primitive, *SOUL.md* as stable persona — precisely the *Endure* axis of SEA's Three Laws. A useful reading: SemaClaw ships the "stability substrate" the SEA survey implicitly assumes a production system must have before you turn Evolve on. One way to stage adoption of SEA's recommendations in a harness like OpenClaw is "SemaClaw floor + Hermes-style procedural-memory evolution on top."

### Compared to Autogenesis ([36](36-autogenesis-self-evolving-agents.md))

Autogenesis is the **protocol layer** that SEA's framework demands but does not prescribe. SEA says "treat every component as optimisable"; Autogenesis answers "then every component needs URI, version, lifecycle, and rollback." RSPL's five resource types (Prompt, Agent, Tool, Environment, Memory) map 1:1 onto SEA's 𝒜 decomposition, and SEPL's Propose→Assess→Commit→Rollback loop is exactly SEA's 𝒜\* search with an explicit *Endure*-enforcing gate. If SEA is the vocabulary, Autogenesis is the first attempt at the syntax.

### Compared to Hyperagents ([45](45-hyperagents-self-modification.md))

Hyperagents are the **MASE limit case** the SEA survey gestures at without naming: a system where *the optimiser itself is in 𝒮*. DGM-H's `improvement_policy` that edits the `meta_policy` that edits the `task_policy` is exactly the recursion SEA implies when it says Law III applies to every component. SEA's Three Laws supply the missing safety framing Hyperagents papers tend to under-specify: Law I (Endure) is what makes population-based search with hard rollback not-optional when the meta-editor is itself editable.

### What SEA gives the practitioner vocabulary didn't have

- A **coverage audit template.** For a given harness, fill in: what's in 𝒮 today? which ℋ is searching it? is Law I (Endure) enforced by a runtime primitive or by convention? You will find most harnesses evolve one dimension (prompts *or* memory *or* workflow) and call it self-improvement. SEA exposes the other axes.
- A **reference vocabulary** for design reviews — "we're MAO, trying to get to MASE on the memory axis first" is a precise statement; "we're self-improving" is not.
- A **safety-gated ordering** — Endure precedes Excel precedes Evolve. Harnesses that ship Evolve without an Endure floor (runtime permission primitives, rollback, auditable lineage) will produce regressions the users blame on the model.

## Limitations and open questions

- **Survey ≠ benchmark.** SEA lists methods; it does not compare them head-to-head on a shared task. Readers who want to know "is AFlow better than DSPy on my workflow?" still have to run both. The survey knows this — Section 4 flags **evaluation methodologies for self-evolving systems** as a research gap, not a solved problem.
- **Snapshot is August 2025.** Anything shipped in 2026 (the Autogenesis protocol, DGM-H hyperagents at scale, OpenClaw-era personal-agent harnesses, Hermes's procedural-memory approach) is not in the taxonomy. The Awesome-list keeps growing; the arxiv paper does not.
- **The four-component framework is descriptive, not generative.** It lets you classify work; it does not tell you how to *compose* optimisers across axes without pathological interactions. Evolving prompts *and* tools *and* topology simultaneously is not the same problem as evolving each in isolation, and the survey acknowledges this as open.
- **Safety formalisation is light.** The Three Laws are a useful rhetorical frame; they are not a formal safety argument. "Maintain safety and stability during modification" is assertional. The hard work — what Law I actually looks like as runtime primitives (cf. PermissionBridge in [54](54-semaclaw-general-purpose-agent.md), SEPL rollback gates in [36](36-autogenesis-self-evolving-agents.md)) — is left to implementers.
- **Cost axis is almost absent.** Continuous optimisation has a token-cost tax that scales with how often ℋ runs. A taxonomy that doesn't foreground this is missing the operational constraint that dominates real deployments.
- **Domain depth is uneven.** Programming and biomedicine get dense coverage; finance/legal are sketched; computer-use and climate are rolled into a single "other" bucket that could each be their own survey.

## Practitioner takeaway

**Copy from this paper:**

1. The **four-component framework** as a design-review template. Before you call a system "self-improving", fill in ℐ, 𝒜, ℰ, 𝒪 explicitly. Most candidates fail at 𝒪 — they have feedback but no optimiser; they have an optimiser but no search space worth exploring.
2. The **Three Laws ordering** — Endure, then Excel, then Evolve. Do not ship Law III without the machinery for Laws I and II. Concretely: evolution without runtime permission gates and rollback is a regression generator.
3. The **MOP→MOA→MAO→MASE** axis as a roadmap, not a marketing ladder. It is fine — often correct — to stop at MAO.
4. The **taxonomy-repo as a reading list.** When picking a technique to add to your harness, browse by cell, read 2–3 primaries, then decide.

**Leave behind:**

1. The **MASE-for-everything impulse.** The survey implicitly celebrates continuous self-modification; in production, *not* evolving most components is the right call for most components.
2. The **Three Laws as a safety story.** They are a frame. Your safety story lives in runtime primitives (permission modes [06](06-permission-modes.md), hooks [05](05-hooks.md), verifier loops [11](11-verifier-evaluator-loops.md)) and in protocol discipline ([36](36-autogenesis-self-evolving-agents.md)), not in three aphorisms.
3. **Benchmark claims lifted from cited methods.** Go to primary sources before quoting them as your own.
4. **The assumption that optimisers compose.** If you want prompt + tool + memory + topology to evolve jointly, plan for joint evaluation; do not assume axis-by-axis wins add up.
