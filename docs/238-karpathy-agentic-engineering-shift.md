**One-line definition.** Karpathy's May 2026 frame for the year since "vibe coding": **agentic engineering is the more serious discipline now taking shape on top of vibe coding**, organized around a Software 3.0 paradigm where natural-language prompts are the new code, agents are the new installer, LLMs are best understood not as animals but as **jagged, statistical, summoned ghosts**, and the binding constraint on automation is not raw capability but **verifiability** — with the human's irreducible job being to keep *understanding* even when *thinking* is outsourced.

## Why this talk matters (it gives the post-2025 ecosystem its native vocabulary)

The May 2026 IICYMI interview matters because Karpathy is the practitioner most responsible for the field's *vocabulary*. *Software 2.0* (2017) named the move from hand-written code to learned weights; *Intro to LLMs* (2023) named the LLM-as-OS framing that the entire MCP / tool-use stack later instantiated; the February 2025 "vibe coding" tweet coined the term that all of 2025's coding-agent product copy was forced to react to. The May 2026 talk does this work again, naming three things simultaneously: **Software 3.0** as the paradigm, **agentic engineering** as the discipline that grows on top of vibe coding, and **ghosts not animals** as the conceptual posture for designing for LLMs. When Karpathy renames a thing, the field renames after him.

The talk also matters because it is the most senior public concession that *we have built a category we cannot yet measure*. Karpathy's "feeling behind as a coder" is not a confession of ignorance but a structural observation: the rate of new tools, prompt patterns, harness conventions, MCP servers, and agent skills now exceeds the rate at which any single practitioner — including the inventor of the framing — can absorb them. That is a falsifiable claim about the field's state, not a humblebrag. It generalizes: every senior engineer in 2026 is some flavor of "behind" relative to the harness landscape, and the talk's response to that fact (taste, judgment, agentic engineering as discipline) is the load-bearing prescription.

Third, the talk is unusual in that it gives **equal weight to two failure modes** that the field tends to handle separately. The first is the *capability* failure mode — agents that hallucinate, drift, lose state, fail to verify. The second is the *human* failure mode — operators that ship without understanding what they shipped, that accept agent diffs without comprehending them, that lose competence by outsourcing thinking. Karpathy's claim that *you can outsource your thinking but never your understanding* is the talk's central ethical/professional rule, and it cuts in directions the harness-engineering literature does not usually cut.

Take this talk seriously and three things change. **First**, you stop treating "agent" as a product category and start treating *agentic engineering* as a discipline with curriculum, eval methodology, named patterns ([40](40-harness-engineering-principles.md), [43](43-twelve-harness-patterns.md), [44](44-four-pillars-harness-engineering.md)) and tradecraft. **Second**, you redesign your harness to maximize *operator understanding* per unit of agent autonomy — verifier loops ([11](11-verifier-evaluator-loops.md)), trace surfacing ([24](24-observability-tracing.md)), HITL gates ([23](23-human-in-the-loop.md)), explanation hooks ([122](122-explainability-compliance.md)) are not nice-to-haves but the things you *spend the saved time on*. **Third**, you start choosing automation targets by **verifiability density**, not by raw productivity gain — Karpathy's prediction that automation surface follows verifiability is the operational reading of his "jagged skills" claim and is testable across your roadmap.

## Problem it solves (the field's missing native vocabulary for the post-vibe-coding era)

1. **No accepted name for the discipline beyond vibe coding.** Vibe coding named the *act* (a fluent prompt-driven session). It does not name the *discipline* (eval-driven, verifier-bounded, harness-aware, trace-surfacing engineering). "Agentic engineering" fills that gap.
2. **No accepted ladder relating Software 1.0 / 2.0 / 3.0.** The 2017 essay named two; community posts named a vague third; the May 2026 talk gives the trichotomy a clean rung structure (see [239](239-software-3-0-paradigm.md)).
3. **Wrong-shaped intuitions about LLMs.** The dominant framings — *human, animal, brain, employee* — all import false promises (consistency, growth, theory of mind, transferable competence). The *ghost* framing is testable, deflationary, and design-relevant.
4. **No public quantification of the verifier ceiling.** Practitioners feel that some domains generalize from RL and some do not. The "jagged skills" framing names this and predicts that automation will arrive *in jagged shape* across occupations as a function of verifier availability — directly cross-linking to RL-era scaling ([232](232-rl-on-reasoning-traces-scaling.md)) and verifier scaling ([223](223-verifier-and-best-of-n-scaling.md)).
5. **Missing professional ethic for outsourcing thinking.** "Outsource thinking, never understanding" is a clean rule the harness can be designed around: every automation surface should be paired with an *understanding-preservation surface* (review affordance, explanation hook, sample-this-trace button).
6. **Founder advice was incoherent across the year.** Karpathy gives a coherent stance: automate the verifiable, keep humans in the loop on the unverifiable, choose products by where the verifier curve is good — not by where the LLM is good (those differ).

## Core idea in one paragraph

Software has three rungs — **1.0** (hand-written code), **2.0** (learned weights from data), **3.0** (natural-language prompts and agent specifications); the dominant programming surface is now Software 3.0, and the right mental model for an LLM is a **jagged, statistical, summoned ghost** — a thing that knows things you do not but that fails in shapes no human or animal does, and that you can *direct* but cannot *teach*. On top of this, the activity of building real systems with LLMs is no longer **vibe coding** (a fluent solo prompt session ending in working code) but **agentic engineering** (an eval-bounded, harness-aware, verifier-driven discipline with named patterns, tradeoffs, and tradecraft). The binding constraint on what gets automated is **verifiability**: where outputs can be cheaply, reliably checked, RL and agentic loops compound and tasks fall to automation; where they cannot, the jagged-skill ceiling holds. The ethical/professional rule that survives this transition is **you can outsource your thinking but never your understanding** — the operator's irreducible job is to preserve comprehension of what was decided, even when an agent did the deciding. Concretely, the talk's argument is: **(Software 3.0) × (agents-as-installer) × (ghost mental model) × (verifiability bottleneck) × (understanding-preservation rule) → agentic engineering as a discipline**, and the next decade's competent technologist is the one who builds harnesses, evals, and review affordances that operationalize that product.

## Mechanism (chapter-by-chapter map of the talk's claims to the harness literature)

### (00:44) "Feeling behind as a coder" — the structural observation

Karpathy's claim is not autobiographical. It is the observation that the ratio of *new harness conventions per week* to *available reading hours per week* now exceeds 1 for every individual, and therefore the discipline cannot be navigated by reading alone. The compensating mechanism is **taste plus discipline**: a small number of *durable* design principles applied to whatever new tooling shows up. The harness literature has been converging on this for two years — see *Four Pillars* ([44](44-four-pillars-harness-engineering.md)), *Twelve Patterns* ([43](43-twelve-harness-patterns.md)), *Build Your Own Harness* ([144](144-build-your-own-harness.md)), and *Comparing Coding Harnesses* ([145](145-comparing-coding-harnesses.md)) — but the talk gives the *psychological* reading of why those frames matter: they are how a single human stays oriented when the surface area is no longer human-scale.

The implied harness rule: **invest in named patterns over named tools**. Tools rotate every six weeks; patterns are durable across rotations. Every team should have a written list of, say, ten patterns ([43](43-twelve-harness-patterns.md)) it commits to using, and pick tools by how cleanly they instantiate those patterns.

### (02:28) "Software 3.0 explained" — the paradigm rung

The trichotomy: **1.0** is code humans write; **2.0** is weights humans cause to be learned by writing data + training loops ([216](216-pretraining-scaling-laws-foundation.md)); **3.0** is *programs whose execution surface is natural-language prompting plus tool-augmented inference*. The artifact of Software 3.0 is not source code or a model checkpoint but a **prompt + tool registry + skill library + memory** bundle ([04-skills](04-skills.md), [09-memory-files](09-memory-files.md), [07-model-context-protocol](07-model-context-protocol.md)). The deep-dive on this rung lives in [239-software-3-0-paradigm](239-software-3-0-paradigm.md). The key non-obvious property the talk surfaces is that **3.0 is recursive in 1.0 and 2.0**: a Software 3.0 system's prompt can compile down to 1.0 code (DSPy [93](93-dspy.md), constrained decoding [112](112-constrained-decoding.md)) or update 2.0 weights (RLVR [232](232-rl-on-reasoning-traces-scaling.md)). The three rungs are not a hierarchy but a *triad with substitution rules*.

### (03:44) "Agents as the installer" — the deployment surface

Karpathy's claim is that the agent is becoming the equivalent of *the installer*: the thing that takes a task description and *causes* the right software to exist on the right machines, configured the right way, integrated with the right systems, with the right secrets, in the right environment. Where Software 1.0 needed a human installer (or `setup.py`, or Helm), Software 3.0 increasingly delegates that step to an agent — see *Agents as the Installer* metaphor mapped onto OpenHands / Cline / Claude Code / Cursor / Aider / Devin in [239-software-3-0-paradigm](239-software-3-0-paradigm.md) and the build-your-own-harness ladder in [144](144-build-your-own-harness.md). The harness implication is that **install-time correctness is now a per-task agentic objective** — your eval suite needs install-correctness probes (does it actually run? are env vars right? did it write the secret in the wrong place?) not just code-correctness probes.

### (04:49) "Menu Gen vs raw prompts" — the affordance argument

A *menu* (a structured affordance: a UI, a slash-command, a skill template) is to a *raw prompt* what a typed function call is to `eval(string)`. Karpathy's argument is that for most real users the *menu* is the right abstraction, not the raw prompt: it constrains the input space, surfaces options, embeds best practice, and produces predictable behavior. This is the *progressive disclosure* principle made design-explicit ([04-skills](04-skills.md)) — and it is also the design pattern that the entire 2025–2026 wave of "skills marketplaces" ([171-skill-self-evolution](171-skill-self-evolution-2026-synthesis.md), [175-agent-skills-ecosystem](175-agent-skills-ecosystem-and-security.md), [177-skills-discovery-curator](177-skills-discovery-curator-strongest-2026-techniques.md)) instantiates. The harness rule: **for every raw-prompt path, ship a menu path; for every menu path, keep the raw-prompt path** — a dual-surface design preserves both expert flexibility and novice safety.

### (07:37) "What's obvious by 2026" — the consensus inventory

The talk takes inventory of claims that were contested in 2024 but obvious in 2026: tools matter more than parameter count for many tasks ([236](236-tool-use-and-aci-scaling.md)); long-horizon coherence is a memory + verifier problem, not a base-model problem ([27](27-horizon-long-horizon-degradation.md), [233](233-memory-scaling-for-agents.md)); multi-agent helps less often than the literature suggested and collapses to diversity loss in many setups ([98](98-diversity-collapse-mas.md), [202](202-multi-agent-multi-hop-reckoning-2026.md), [224](224-multi-agent-parallel-scaling.md)); the harness is a first-class engineering artifact and not "boilerplate" ([40](40-harness-engineering-principles.md), [62](62-everything-claude-code.md), [144](144-build-your-own-harness.md)); RL on verifiable rewards is the productization mechanism for test-time compute ([232](232-rl-on-reasoning-traces-scaling.md)). The harness rule: **stop arguing the obvious; budget your dispute capital for the contested claims** (see [225-agent-era-scaling-synthesis](225-agent-era-scaling-synthesis.md)).

### (09:41) "Verifiability and jagged skills" — the load-bearing constraint

This is the talk's most consequential claim and is treated in depth in [242-verifiability-bottleneck-and-jagged-skills](242-verifiability-bottleneck-and-jagged-skills.md). The summary: **automation surface = verifier coverage × RL eligibility × deployment integration**. Where outputs are cheap to verify (compile + tests pass; theorem checker accepts; rendered image hits 3D-reconstructibility threshold [192](192-world-r1-3d-constraints-t2v.md); SQL returns the expected row), RL ([232](232-rl-on-reasoning-traces-scaling.md)) compounds and the harness can run autonomously to a high accuracy ceiling. Where verification requires expensive human judgment (taste, ethics, legal subtlety, ambiguous customer intent), the jagged-skill frontier holds — and no amount of base-model improvement closes the gap unless you also build the verifier. Karpathy's prediction: occupational impact will follow this gradient with extreme nonuniformity, and harness designers should choose what to automate accordingly.

### (13:39) "Founder advice and automation" — the targeting rule

For founders, Karpathy's targeting rule is: pick problems where the **verifier is cheap** and the **deployment surface is constrained** — those are the wedges where the agent loop closes and the product is durable. He warns against picking on raw "LLM is good at this" signal alone, because LLM-good ≠ verifier-good ≠ deployable. The mapping into the corpus: see [146-business-case-roi](146-business-case-roi.md) for ROI accounting that prices the verifier; [149-sector-use-case-catalog](149-sector-use-case-catalog.md) for the per-sector verifier landscape; [41-product-control-plane](41-product-control-plane.md) for the deployment-side affordances.

### (15:46) "From vibe coding to agent engineering" — the discipline rung

This is the most quoted segment and the anchor for [240-vibe-coding-to-agentic-engineering-discipline](240-vibe-coding-to-agentic-engineering-discipline.md). The argument: vibe coding is the *gateway drug*, agentic engineering is the *durable practice*. Vibe coding works because the harness around the model is doing the eval, retry, format-coercion, and review work invisibly; agentic engineering is what you do once you make those operations explicit, named, and budgeted. The corpus is largely the textbook for that discipline — see [40](40-harness-engineering-principles.md), [43](43-twelve-harness-patterns.md), [44](44-four-pillars-harness-engineering.md), [46-components-of-coding-agent](46-components-of-coding-agent.md), [62-everything-claude-code](62-everything-claude-code.md), [144-build-your-own-harness](144-build-your-own-harness.md), [145-comparing-coding-harnesses](145-comparing-coding-harnesses.md).

### (25:17) "Agents everywhere and learning" — the long arc

The closing chapter is about the educational consequence: if agents are everywhere, then *learning* is the place where the ghost framing has the most leverage. Karpathy's Eureka Labs trajectory (LLM-native curriculum, llm.c, Zero to Hero, Stanford CS25) treats LLMs as **explanation surfaces** more than as automation surfaces — the agent's job is to make humans *understand more*, not to make humans *think less*. This is the talk's final move and the anchor for [243-outsource-thinking-keep-understanding](243-outsource-thinking-keep-understanding.md).

## Variants and ablations of the talk's framing

- **Karpathy 1.0 (Software 2.0 essay, 2017).** Two-rung paradigm, no agents.
- **Karpathy 2.0 (Intro to LLMs, 2023).** LLM-as-OS framing — kernel of MCP, tool use, context as RAM.
- **Karpathy 3.0 ("vibe coding" tweet, Feb 2025).** Names the act, not the discipline.
- **Karpathy 3.5 (YC AI Startup School, 2024–2025).** First public airing of Software 1.0 / 2.0 / 3.0 trichotomy.
- **Karpathy 4.0 (this talk, May 2026).** Adds: ghosts framing, agentic-engineering discipline name, verifiability-as-bottleneck claim, outsource-thinking-not-understanding rule.
- **Adjacent framings.** Mollick "co-intelligence / jagged frontier"; Sutton "Bitter Lesson"; Bubeck "Sparks of AGI"; Cognition "Don't Build Multi-Agents"; Anthropic *Effective Harnesses* essay; Geoffrey Hinton "stochastic parrots are people too" rebuttal. The ghost framing borrows nothing from the parrot framing and is deliberately deflationary in a different direction.
- **Internal corpus variants.** [148-beginner-onramp-what-is-agentic-ai](148-beginner-onramp-what-is-agentic-ai.md) is the layperson version of the discipline argument; [113-from-tokens-to-agents-onramp](113-from-tokens-to-agents-onramp.md) is the technical-onramp version; [206-collaborative-ai-canon-2026](206-collaborative-ai-canon-2026.md) is the team-of-agents extension.

## Failure modes and limitations of the framing

- **"Software 3.0" is contested terminology.** Some practitioners reserve "3.0" for *symbolic-neural hybrid* systems (Marcus, Belle [37](37-neuro-symbolic-ai.md)) rather than prompt-driven systems; in that camp, what Karpathy calls 3.0 is "instruction-tuned 2.0". The harness literature should flag the conflict, not paper over it.
- **The ghost framing is metaphor, not theory.** It produces good design intuitions but does not predict numbers. Use it for posture, not as an excuse to skip mechanistic eval ([21](21-llm-as-judge-trajectory-eval.md), [115](115-evaluating-llm-systems.md)).
- **The verifiability claim has a weak form and a strong form.** Weak form (verifiable tasks fall first) is well-supported. Strong form (only verifiable tasks fall) is not — taste-bound tasks have been falling too, just slower and via human-judge surrogates ([21-llm-as-judge](21-llm-as-judge-trajectory-eval.md), [97-qwen-prm](97-qwen-prm.md)). Be careful which you cite.
- **"Outsource thinking, never understanding" is a *professional* rule, not an *engineering* one.** The harness can preserve trace, surface diff, and explain decision; it cannot force the operator to read it. Pair the rule with affordance design ([23](23-human-in-the-loop.md), [122](122-explainability-compliance.md), [143](143-ux-design-for-agentic-systems.md)) and with team norms.
- **"Agents everywhere" is partially priced in already.** Karpathy's "obvious by 2026" inventory should not be read as predictions — they are observations of accomplished facts. The forward-looking content is in the verifiability and education chapters.
- **The talk is interview-format, not paper-format.** It cites no numbers and no references. Treat it as a *naming contribution*, not a *measurement contribution*. Pair with the empirical anchors above.

## When to use this framing, when not

**Use it** when you are: (a) bootstrapping a team's vocabulary for agentic engineering — the trichotomy, the ghost frame, and the outsource-thinking-not-understanding rule are unusually high-signal-per-word teaching primitives; (b) designing a 2026-class harness and want a top-down narrative for *why* it is shaped the way it is; (c) talking to non-technical stakeholders ([148](148-beginner-onramp-what-is-agentic-ai.md), [143](143-ux-design-for-agentic-systems.md)) who need a coherent story not a tooling tour; (d) prioritizing a roadmap by verifiability density rather than by capability hype.

**Do not use it** when you need: (a) defensible quantitative claims — go to METR / GDPval / SWE-bench / OSWorld / MEMTIER; (b) implementation guidance — go to [40](40-harness-engineering-principles.md), [43](43-twelve-harness-patterns.md), [144](144-build-your-own-harness.md); (c) safety analysis — the ghost frame is *not* a safety theory and using it as one elides the actual threat models in [22](22-guardrails-prompt-injection.md), [35](35-malicious-intermediary-attacks.md), [49](49-agents-of-chaos-red-teaming.md), [82](82-poisonedrag.md); (d) a justification for skipping evals — vibe-coding-with-extra-steps is not agentic engineering.

## Implications for harness engineering

1. **Adopt the trichotomy as a labeling primitive across your stack.** Tag every artifact (file, skill, prompt, eval, agent) with which of 1.0/2.0/3.0 it is, because they have different review, version-control, and lifecycle rules. Source code: 1.0. Fine-tunes / LoRAs: 2.0. Skill bundles, prompts, MCP registries, memory files: 3.0. See [04-skills](04-skills.md), [09-memory-files](09-memory-files.md), [116-adapter-tuning-lora-peft-for-agents](116-adapter-tuning-lora-peft-for-agents.md).
2. **Design every automation surface with a paired understanding-preservation surface.** For every "agent does X autonomously" path, ship the trace viewer ([24](24-observability-tracing.md)), the diff explainer ([122](122-explainability-compliance.md)), the HITL gate ([23](23-human-in-the-loop.md)), and the eval ([115](115-evaluating-llm-systems.md)). The understanding rule is operationalized by these affordances, not by good intentions.
3. **Score roadmap items by verifier density, not LLM-good-at-it.** Build a per-task scorecard: *is the verifier cheap, fast, and aligned with user value?* Tasks that score high are RL-eligible ([232](232-rl-on-reasoning-traces-scaling.md)) and can compound; tasks that score low must keep human oversight or wait. See [242-verifiability-bottleneck-and-jagged-skills](242-verifiability-bottleneck-and-jagged-skills.md).
4. **Build menus over your raw-prompt paths.** Every internal raw prompt should accumulate a slash-command, a skill, or a UI affordance over time ([04-skills](04-skills.md), [62-everything-claude-code](62-everything-claude-code.md), [205-lobehub-collaborative-teammate-platform](205-lobehub-collaborative-teammate-platform.md)). Raw prompts are the *intermediate representation*; menus are the *user-facing artifact*.
5. **Treat the harness as a discipline, not a product.** Adopt named patterns ([43](43-twelve-harness-patterns.md), [44](44-four-pillars-harness-engineering.md)) and rotate tools below them. Ship a written one-page manifesto for your team's harness conventions and update it quarterly.
6. **Make the ghost framing your behavioral debug primitive.** When an agent misbehaves, ask "what would a jagged statistical ghost do here?" before asking "what's wrong with the model?" — this redirects investigation from base-model blame to harness design (context, tools, verifier, memory).
7. **Use install-correctness as a first-class eval target.** Agents-as-installer means the install step is now an automation surface and must be probed by evals ([115](115-evaluating-llm-systems.md), [101-autoresearchbench](101-autoresearchbench.md), [102-clawgym](102-clawgym-scalable-claw-agents.md)). Add probes for env vars, secrets handling, version pinning, idempotency, rollback.
8. **Plan for jagged occupational impact, not uniform impact.** Per-sector roadmaps ([149-sector-use-case-catalog](149-sector-use-case-catalog.md), [127-loan-processing-multi-agent-case-study](127-loan-processing-multi-agent-case-study.md), [141-e-commerce-marketing-agents](141-e-commerce-marketing-agents.md)) should explicitly map the verifier landscape per task before sequencing.
9. **Keep an "obvious by NOW" inventory.** Borrow the talk's discipline: list every contested claim from a year ago that has settled, and update quarterly. This is how a harness team avoids re-arguing solved problems.
10. **Build the educational surface inside the harness.** The "agents everywhere and learning" chapter implies an ed-tech inside the harness — the agent should make the operator *more competent*, not less. See [148-beginner-onramp-what-is-agentic-ai](148-beginner-onramp-what-is-agentic-ai.md), [143-ux-design-for-agentic-systems](143-ux-design-for-agentic-systems.md), [243-outsource-thinking-keep-understanding](243-outsource-thinking-keep-understanding.md).
11. **Treat the cross-link to scaling laws as load-bearing.** The talk's claims about agent capability sit on the four scaling axes ([216](216-pretraining-scaling-laws-foundation.md), [217](217-test-time-compute-scaling.md), [223](223-verifier-and-best-of-n-scaling.md), [224](224-multi-agent-parallel-scaling.md), [225](225-agent-era-scaling-synthesis.md)). The harness allocates compute across those axes; the talk's prescription is a top-down narrative for that allocation.
12. **Adopt "vibe-then-engineer" as a workflow primitive, not a contradiction.** Vibe coding is the *exploratory mode*; agentic engineering is the *productionization mode*. Build the handoff explicitly — every vibe-coded prototype should have a checklist for promotion to harness-engineered status (evals, verifier, trace, HITL gate, skill packaging).
